# The Loop Runs Faster Than Thought

Geoffrey Litt’s essay, *Understanding is the new bottleneck*, begins with a claim that sounds almost quaint in the age of agents: humans still need to understand the code that AI writes. But the deeper point is not about code review. It is about participation. Litt argues that understanding matters not only so we can verify what the machine did, but so we can remain active creative participants in the next turn of the work. He proposes techniques like AI-generated explainers, quizzes, micro-worlds, and shared spaces as ways to help humans keep up with agent-made systems.  [oai_citation:0‡geoffreylitt.com](https://www.geoffreylitt.com/2026/07/02/understanding-is-the-new-bottleneck.html?utm_source=tldrnewsletter)

That distinction matters.

Verification is a checkpoint. Participation is a relationship.

For the last few years, much of the conversation around AI has been organized around removal. Remove the tedious parts. Remove the boilerplate. Remove the waiting. Remove the human from the loop.

And in some places, this is exactly right. There are loops that humans should not have to occupy. Nobody should spend their best attention renaming files, translating obvious API calls, or manually copying data from one format to another. Automation, at its best, gives attention back.

But there is another kind of loop. A loop where the point is not merely to produce an output, but to form a mental model. A loop where each pass changes not only the artifact, but the person making it. A loop where understanding accumulates.

These are the loops we should be careful about leaving.

Because a loop you leave is no longer a loop you can steer.

## Cognitive debt

Litt invokes the idea of cognitive debt: the debt you take on when a system advances faster than your understanding of it. It is like technical debt, except the mess is not only in the codebase. It is in the relationship between the codebase and the people responsible for it.  [oai_citation:1‡geoffreylitt.com](https://www.geoffreylitt.com/2026/07/02/understanding-is-the-new-bottleneck.html?utm_source=tldrnewsletter)

Technical debt says: this still runs, but changing it later will hurt.

Cognitive debt says: this still runs, but I no longer know what game I am playing.

That is the subtler danger of AI-generated work. Not that it will always be wrong. Often it will be right enough. Sometimes it will be excellent. The danger is that the artifact will move forward while the human remains conceptually behind.

The pull request gets merged. The app compiles. The demo works. The agent reports success.

But the person who is supposed to own the next decision has only a thin understanding of what now exists.

This is not just a quality problem. It is an authorship problem.

When you do not understand the system, you can still ask for changes. But your asks become smaller, vaguer, and more reactive. You can request fixes. You can complain about outputs. You can point to symptoms.

What you cannot easily do is imagine the next shape.

Understanding is what lets you make non-obvious moves. It gives you the internal vocabulary to say, “Actually, this wants to become something else.” It lets you feel which changes are cheap, which are dangerous, which are elegant, which are evasions. It lets you collaborate with the machine as a medium instead of treating it as a vending machine.

That is why the bottleneck has moved.

For a long time, the scarce thing was production. Could we make the thing? Could we write enough code, design enough screens, generate enough options, explore enough possibilities?

Now the machine can produce more than we can metabolize.

So the scarce thing becomes orientation.

## The strange loop of AI work

There is a recursive twist here that feels important.

We use AI to build systems. Then we use AI to explain those systems. Then we use AI to build little worlds in which we can interact with those systems, test our understanding, and discover what to ask next.

The machine generates the artifact.

Then the machine generates the map.

Then the machine generates the classroom.

Then, inside that classroom, the human tries to become capable again.

This is the strange loop of AI work: intelligence producing the conditions for another intelligence to remain meaningfully involved.

That is a very different story from “the AI does the work.”

It is closer to: the AI changes the shape of the work, and then must help us change the shape of our understanding.

Litt’s examples are practical, but they point to something larger. He describes code explainers that do not simply dump a diff, but teach background concepts, build intuition before details, and arrange changes in a more comprehensible order. He describes quizzes that force the human to check whether they actually understood the change. He describes micro-worlds: interactive environments, debuggers, and command centers that let a person inhabit a system instead of merely inspect it.  [oai_citation:2‡geoffreylitt.com](https://www.geoffreylitt.com/2026/07/02/understanding-is-the-new-bottleneck.html?utm_source=tldrnewsletter)

These are not just productivity tricks.

They are new interfaces for cognition.

The old interface was the artifact itself: read the code, inspect the diff, run the program, stare at the logs. The new interface may be an explainer, a simulation, a quiz, a visualization, a generated debugger, a temporary teaching environment.

In other words: the system no longer arrives alone. It arrives with a pedagogical wrapper.

That wrapper may become one of the most important parts of software.

## Friction as a feature

One of Litt’s most useful phrases is that a quiz can act as a speed regulator. When the AI loop moves faster than human understanding, the quiz adds a deliberate constraint: do I actually understand this well enough to continue?  [oai_citation:3‡geoffreylitt.com](https://www.geoffreylitt.com/2026/07/02/understanding-is-the-new-bottleneck.html?utm_source=tldrnewsletter)

This is interesting because it cuts against the dominant aesthetic of AI tools.

Most AI products are sold as acceleration. Faster writing. Faster coding. Faster research. Faster design. Faster everything.

But mature AI work may require designed slowness.

Not slowness everywhere. Not bureaucratic drag. Not ceremony for its own sake. But moments where the system refuses to let fluency cosplay as understanding.

A good AI tool might sometimes say: before we continue, explain this back.

Or: choose between these two mental models.

Or: here is a tiny simulation; predict what happens before you run it.

Or: you accepted this change, but your explanation suggests you do not yet understand its consequence.

That kind of friction will feel annoying if we think the goal is simply throughput. But it becomes essential if the goal is sustained agency.

The point is not to slow the machine down because we resent its speed. The point is to keep the human from being quietly ejected from the process.

Aviation has checklists. Science has peer review. Law has procedure. Education has exercises. Every serious domain invents rituals that interrupt raw action in order to preserve judgment.

AI work will need its own rituals.

## Micro-worlds and moral orientation

The most evocative of Litt’s techniques is the micro-world: a small interactive environment that lets someone build intuition by playing with a system.

This idea reaches beyond programming.

Imagine a policy team using AI to draft a new housing regulation. The bottleneck is not producing a document. The bottleneck is understanding the second-order effects: incentives, loopholes, edge cases, tradeoffs, winners, losers. A micro-world could let the team simulate households, landlords, inspectors, budgets, delays, and failure modes.

Imagine a doctor reviewing an AI-suggested treatment plan. The bottleneck is not the generated recommendation. The bottleneck is understanding why this patient, with this history, under these constraints, should follow this path. A micro-world could expose the assumptions and possible branches.

Imagine a school district adopting an AI tutoring system. The bottleneck is not whether the software can generate exercises. The bottleneck is whether teachers, students, and parents understand what kind of learning environment is being created.

In each case, the question is not simply: did the AI produce something?

The question is: did the surrounding humans become more capable of judgment?

This is where “understanding” becomes more than a cognitive virtue. It becomes an ethical one.

A person who does not understand a system may still be legally responsible for it. A manager may approve it. A developer may merge it. A doctor may sign off on it. A citizen may live under it.

But responsibility without understanding is a hollow ritual.

If AI systems are going to act at increasing scale, then we need tools that do not merely produce decisions, artifacts, and plans. We need tools that improve the human capacity to inhabit those decisions before they harden into reality.

## The return of augmentation

The cleanest line in Litt’s essay may be his reminder that the point was always to augment, not just automate.  [oai_citation:4‡geoffreylitt.com](https://www.geoffreylitt.com/2026/07/02/understanding-is-the-new-bottleneck.html?utm_source=tldrnewsletter)

Automation removes a task.

Augmentation expands a person.

The difference is easy to blur because both can involve the same technology. An AI assistant can write the code for you, or it can help you understand the code you are writing. It can replace a step, or it can deepen your relationship to the whole process. It can make you faster while making you thinner, or it can make you faster while making you stronger.

The market tends to reward the first story because it is easier to measure. How many minutes saved? How many tickets closed? How many words generated? How many agents deployed?

But the second story may matter more.

How much richer is the team’s shared mental model?

How much more quickly can a newcomer understand the system?

How much more creatively can someone propose the next change?

How much less afraid are people to touch the thing?

How much more agency does the user have after the machine has acted?

These are harder metrics. They are also closer to the heart of the matter.

## The bottleneck is participation

The fear of AI is often framed as replacement. The hope of AI is often framed as leisure. The reality may be stranger than either.

AI may give us more to do, not less, because it will expand the frontier of what can be attempted. It will create more drafts, more prototypes, more simulations, more strategies, more codebases, more possible worlds.

The old bottleneck was making.

The new bottleneck is understanding what has been made well enough to choose what should be made next.

That means the future may not belong to the people who can make machines do things. That part is getting easier. It may belong to the people and teams who can stay meaningfully oriented while machines do many things at once.

The bottleneck is no longer production.

The bottleneck is participation.

And participation requires understanding.

Not total understanding. Not the fantasy of omniscience. No one understands every layer of the systems they use. But enough understanding to ask good questions. Enough to notice when the shape is wrong. Enough to feel the grain of the material. Enough to carry intention from one loop to the next.

This is the work ahead: not merely building agents that can act, but building environments in which humans can remain awake inside the action.

The machine can generate the code.

The machine can generate the explanation.

The machine can generate the little world where we learn what the code means.

But the human still has to undergo the understanding.

That may turn out to be the most valuable work of all.
